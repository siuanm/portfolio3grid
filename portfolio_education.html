I smiled, — for what had I to fear?

Next Project

This series of twelve portraits, created with digitally composited photography, delves into the intricate interplay between contemporary, performative gender roles and their historical roots in "women's work" within the domestic sphere.

By juxtaposing modern identity constructs with echoes of traditional labor and spaces, the work interrogates how societal expectations shape — and are shaped by — our evolving notions of femininity, labor, and place.

Three prints are held in the National Collection of Ireland.

About

I am an artist, designer, and educator working across digital and material media. My practice explores the relationships between technology, perception, and human experience, often situated at the intersection of creative experimentation and critical inquiry.

With a professional background spanning visual effects, simulation & 3D modeling at NASA-JPL, and product design in complex domains, I bring an applied perspective to my teaching and research. I am particularly interested in how systems—technical, visual, and cultural—shape the way we interpret and interact with the world.

My teaching centers on helping students develop both conceptual rigor and technical fluency, with an emphasis on digital imaging, user experience and emerging technologies. I encourage approaches that are exploratory, reflective, and grounded in human-centered and ethical considerations.

My studio practice operates in parallel with my academic work, informing a pedagogy that values experimentation, interdisciplinary thinking, and the integration of art and design as modes of research.

Teaching Statement

My teaching is grounded in a simple goal: to spark curiosity. At a time when information is everywhere, I see my role less as delivering content and more as creating the conditions for engagement. I want students to develop the interest, confidence, and critical thinking skills to continue learning on their own, and to feel a sense of ownership over that process.

This perspective is shaped by my background in UX design, where understanding human needs is central. I bring that same mindset into the classroom by designing learning experiences around students and their perspectives, strengths, and goals. Getting to know students as individuals is a necessary starting point. When that foundation of trust is in place, students are more willing to take risks, participate in critique, and push their thinking beyond what feels comfortable.

From there, I focus on structure. I design courses with scaffolding in mind, creating multiple entry points while guiding students toward greater complexity and independence. Frameworks such as Universal Design for Learning and Bloom’s Taxonomy inform how I build assignments and assessments, but I use them as flexible guides rather than rigid systems. Students engage in different ways, and they should have different ways to demonstrate what they know. This approach allows me to support a wide range of learners, including differences in cultural background, neurodiversity, prior experience, and individual interests.

At the same time, I want the classroom to feel active and alive. Creative media education should be engaging, and ideally, enjoyable. I want students to come to class because they are invested in the work, not simply because they have to be there. Whether projects are oriented toward professional application or grounded in critical art practice, I emphasize experimentation, iteration, and a willingness to explore alongside technical skill-building. The goal is not just competency, but momentum. Students should leave with the sense that they want to keep going.

That kind of environment depends on a real commitment to equity and access. I am especially interested in supporting students from economically diverse backgrounds and in helping to bridge gaps in access to creative and technical education. I work to create a space that is welcoming and supportive, where students feel that they belong and have what they need to succeed.

My approach is informed by a range of experiences across different learning environments, including community college study, advanced coursework at UCLA, graduate study in Ireland, formal educator training in Vermont, and ongoing participation in industry workshops and professional development. Moving between these contexts has reinforced my belief that there is no single way to teach effectively. I draw from multiple approaches and adjust based on the students in front of me and the goals of the course. My experience teaching both emerging learners and industry professionals, including my role as Advisory Faculty at Deloitte where I earned Facilitator Excellence certification, has further shaped how I design interactive, engaging learning experiences.

I think of teaching as a form of design practice. It is iterative, empathetic, and collaborative. My goal is to create a classroom where students feel challenged but supported, and where they leave not only with new skills, but with a lasting sense of curiosity, confidence, and creative possibility.
